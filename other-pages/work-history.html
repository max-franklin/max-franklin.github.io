Anchorage Landfill Drone Feasibility Study

With the expanding use of drones for performing services such as photography, geographic mapping, cargo transport, etc, it's not surprising that municipalities such as Anchorage, Alaska are looking for ways to apply drone technology to cut costs. Working under Dr. Aaron Doston with the University of Alaska Anchorage, I had the responsibility of analyzing photogrammetric data collected by K2Dronotics to calculate volume differences over weekly time frames at the Anchorage Landfill. Volume differences can be used to determine the amount of trash being deposited at the landfill, and how much cover (gravel, wood) is being laid over the trash. Furthermore, as the landfill crew compacts the trash down, a baseline rate of compaction can be determined from the volume differences, ultimately allowing for an estimate on landfill lifespan. To analyze incoming photogrammetry data, I heavily utilized the Numpy library to grid the data and perform a Reimann summation for estimating volume differences on a weekly basis. Matplotlib was used for data visualization including 3d point cloud and contour plot construction. The ultimate outcome was the demonstration of drone aerial photogrammetry as being an efficient method for producing rough estimates of landfill volume changes in timely manner.

Subsistence Hunting Agent-Based Model

As global climate change pressures caribou populations across the Arctic, increasing human development further restricts both available caribou habitat and subsistence hunting grounds. I am currently working with Dr. Martin Cenek at the University of Alaska Anchorage to understand caribou behavior dynamics through agent-based modeling (ABM), a powerful modeling technique relying on anonymous agents with restricted logical decision-making capabilities (akin to humans). The enforcement of non-omnipotence on the agents in ABMs results in agents interacting with one another in complex ways similar to how individuals interact with each other in real life. Indepedent agents arive at decisions through assessing their state and situation. For caribou agents, state could be energy level and situation the quality of habitat immediately available. Agents also have the capability to adapt to changing environments through the machine-learning technique of genetic algorithms (GAs). GAs embody the concept of Darwinian evolution, with agents that enjoy a greater degree of success being more likely to survive and pass on their decision making characteristics. The ultimate objective of this project is to simulate different development and climate change scenarios on the North Slope of Alaska to understand how subsistence hunting might be impacted. Currently, the project is nearing completion, with several publications currently in the works.

Kenai River Fisheries Agent-Based Model

Global climate change poses a significant threat to salmon fisheries. As streams and oceans warm, salmon mortality increases and salmon behaviors undergo shifts in an attempt to adapt. In Southeast Alaska, the impact of a warming climate has led to altered start and duration of migration timing for returning salmon species. The Kenai River may be next to experience such temporal shifts in migration timing, potentially disturbing the complex dynamics of the commercial and personal-use fisheries present there. Complicating the matter, the river has two different species of salmon returning at the same time: a large run (~ 2-6 million) of Oncorhynchus nerka (sockeye salmon) along with a much smaller run (~ 30,000) of Oncorhynchus tshawytscha (Chinook salmon). The vastly different sizes of these runs paired with their concurrency necessitates complex management decisions opening and closing the fisheries to try and maximize sockeye salmon harvest and minimize Chinook salmon by-catch. As the peak run timing of these returning salmon species shifts, social dynamics such as personal-use fishery participation and commerical fishery gear regulation will shift. To understand the net outcome of the different possible scenarios at the Kenai River, I constructed an ABM to serve as a simulation platform for conducting scenario-based studies. My work has resulted in a published masters thesis and three journal publications in collaboration with Dr. Martin Cenek. For greater detail on the matter, please refer to the publications below.
